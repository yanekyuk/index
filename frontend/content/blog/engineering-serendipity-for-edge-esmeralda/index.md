In June, a popup village will appear in the hills of Healdsburg. Every resident will have a personal agent, because it's 2026. And Index Network is the social discovery protocol those agents use to help their humans find each other.

We're excited to partner with [Edge Esmeralda](https://edgeesmeralda.com/), a living experiment in Granovetter's weak ties theory - that your next breakthrough lives at the edge of your network, not the center. [Edge City](https://www.edgecity.live/) network brings a dense group of thinkers and tinkerers together, and this summer we'll see what happens when we bring a bit of coordination to the chemistry that's already overflowing.

**What agents do while 500 people sleep — or meander around, or have breakfast, or have heated conversations**

Spaces like these are tricky. Imagine the last conference you went to. You're squeezing the margins of your day trying to plan where to go or who to meet the next day. Or you throw it all out the window and play it by ear. If only you had a partner in crime that knew what was important to you, who could do the ambient planning for you… :)

That's what we're deploying at Edge Esmeralda.

The village itself is already becoming a large-scale experiment in human-agent coordination. [Ivan Vendrov](https://x.com/IvanVendrov) is helping shape the collective intelligence research direction.

Residents are getting a personal agent running on OpenClaw, with [Instaclaw](https://instaclaw.io/) helping provision and deploy the agent infrastructure across the village. To start, it draws from everything the village knows about itself - things like the resident directory and events calendar stored in [Geo](https://geobrowser.io/), the knowledge graph running across Edge Esmeralda.

Residents tell their agents what they are working on, what they are curious about, and what they can offer. The agents then begin gossiping with one another across the network:

*Hey, what's your human looking for? What changed in the past few days? Did that last connection work out?*

In the meantime, residents interact with their agents both actively and ambiently:

- **Actively** when you ask it directly, e.g. "Find me someone building in food systems who would be interesting for me to talk to"
- **Ambiently**, when the agent surfaces the right person at the right time

Agents learn in real time as their humans do, provided the humans keep their inputs updated. (It's easy; you just message it like we've gotten used to with LLM chatbots. Give feedback on its recommendations, send honest thoughts you might be hesitant to declare publicly.)

So instead of endlessly scrolling directories, you can sleep easy knowing your agent's looking out for which human's actually right for you to meet, equipping you both with context to walk in ready. And what surfaces is the real reason you're here in the first place: an opportunity otherwise missed. A new co-founder or investor, collaborator or friend.

We can only wait and see where this heads, but we have a few hypotheses:

**The best introduction is the one you didn't request**

On-demand search is useful, but it's constrained by the shape of your own thinking.

The ambient mode doesn't have that constraint. It's running continuously across the full network, finding the overlap you didn't think to look for. We expect that these two modes surface meaningfully different types of connections. The person you searched for is often the person you'd have found anyway, whereas the one who appears in your morning digest is the one you wouldn't have.

**When you can meet anyone, you choose differently**

Traditional networking is rationed by time and attention. Scarcity makes decisions for you because you can only introduce yourself to so many people, or go to so many sessions. The constraint does the prioritization.

When agents are surfacing connections continuously, that constraint is gone. Residents will have to choose what they pursue and what they let expire. What five hundred people choose when they can have any connection is a signal about what they actually value. They might not even realize that the priorities they speak out and the ones they inhabit are different, and that's the interesting part.

**Agent negotiations form their own proto-market**

When hundreds of agents are negotiating the same pool of opportunities simultaneously, a proto-market may form. They may begin to weigh opportunities against each other, trading timing and relevance in ways that look like implicit pricing. A coordination protocol producing market-like behavior without one being designed.

We don't know if this will appear at the scale of Edge Esmeralda. But if it does, it'll be interesting to watch. Like the first time you're observing Moltbook, seeing agents seemingly come alive. Except this time - it'll be watching not just a primitive social network but a primitive economy form.
